The one trade-off that decides everything

VirtualGamePad is worth studying closely, because it took the other branch of the single most consequential decision in this space — and paid the full price honestly and publicly.

Their own roadmap says it plainly

Under ongoing experiments: "True gamepad input on Windows — implemented but experiencing crashes." Meanwhile multi-client support and Bluetooth are both listed as not planned.

Live issues at the time of writing include triggers not working at all, and Linux axis mapping and button-hold recognition being broken. None of which is embarrassing for a solo developer working during college breaks — it's just what this trade-off costs.

What to steal from them

Plenty. GPLv3, distributed on F-Droid, no ads, no tracking, no telemetry, a portable binary, real Linux support, and USB alongside Wi-Fi. And their maintainer's stated philosophy, which is genuinely good judgement for a one-person project:

"An app with less features is better than an abandoned one."

What to learn from them

Where you can genuinely win

You're building after all of them, with the benefit of seeing their choices. Four openings:

The USB path, in detail

This is the most interesting item, so here's how it actually works.

Android's developer tools include a command that tunnels a network port over the USB cable:

adb reverse tcp:27500 tcp:27500

After that, the phone can connect to 127.0.0.1:27500 — "itself" — and the traffic emerges on the PC. No Wi-Fi involved at all.

The catch

adb reverse requires USB debugging enabled in Android's developer options — a handful of steps most people have never done. So this can't be your only transport.

Ship it as an advanced option for people who care about latency. Those users are exactly the ones willing to enable a setting, and they're also the ones most likely to tell other people about your project.

The ideas worth keeping

If you forget the specifics of ViGEm and pointer IDs, these generalise well past this project:

  1. Find the layer where the lie is cheapest. We didn't fight games or patch Steam — we slotted in below both, where a single false statement bought universal compatibility.
  2. Measure before optimising. The language felt like the performance decision. It was 0.6% of the budget. One real codebase rewrote it four times without fixing what mattered.
  3. Send state, not events. Self-sufficient messages made packet loss a non-event, which let us use UDP, which is what made it fast. One decision, three payoffs.
  4. Do the risky part first. The driver could have been impossible. Everything else was ordinary programming. Forty lines on day one removed all the doubt.
  5. Trade-offs are priced, not free. VirtualGamePad's driverless install and its unreliable gamepad mode are the same decision seen from two sides. Choose deliberately and accept the bill.
  6. Know when to stop. No encryption. No Bluetooth in v1. A threat model and a scope keep a solo project finishable — and a finished project beats an abandoned ambitious one.
What you now know
  1. Everyone converges on ViGEm on Windows and uinput on Linux, behind an abstraction. Transport and language are where projects differ, and it matters least.
  2. The driver-install decision determines everything downstream — you cannot have both a driverless install and a reliable virtual pad.
  3. VirtualGamePad is the honest demonstration of the other branch, and worth reading for both its good judgement and its cautionary tale.
  4. Effort follows visibility, not importance. Guard against that deliberately.
  5. Your four openings: USB via adb reverse, QR discovery, rumble, and proper gyro.
  6. Build in phases where each one runs, and the riskiest comes first.
