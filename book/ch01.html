Chapter One gentle

What are we actually building?

Before any code, we need to agree on what the goal is — and see why it's harder than "make the phone send button presses."

Assumed knowledge: none. If you've plugged in a game controller before, you're qualified.

The goal, in one sentence

You have an old Android phone in a drawer. You want to pick it up, tap a button on its screen, and have your character jump in a game running on your Windows PC — with the game treating that phone as a completely ordinary Xbox controller.

That's it. That's the whole product. But sitting inside that sentence is a surprising amount of machinery, and the goal of this book is that by the end you'll understand every piece of it.

Two halves and a gap

Whatever else happens, the system has to have two programs in it, because there are two devices.

Client and server

These words sound grand but mean something simple. The server is the program that sits there waiting, listening. The client is the program that reaches out and starts the conversation.

Your phone is the client because it's the one that goes looking for a PC. The PC is the server because it waits to be found. That's the only reason those names apply — it has nothing to do with which machine is more powerful.

What a controller actually is, to a computer

Here's the first thing that trips people up. When you imagine a gamepad, you probably picture events: "A got pressed," "the stick moved left." That's how it feels to use one.

But that's not what a controller sends. A controller doesn't announce events. It answers a question, over and over, forever, hundreds of times a second. The question is: "what does your entire body look like right now?"

Think of it this way

An event-based controller is like a friend narrating a football match over the phone: "he's passing… he's shooting…" If the line cuts out for two seconds, you've permanently lost that information. You have to ask them to catch you up.

A state-based controller is like a live video feed. If it glitches for two seconds, you don't need catching up — the next frame shows you exactly where everything is. Nothing was lost, because every frame is complete on its own.

Analog vs digital

Digital means only two possible values — on or off. A button is digital. A keyboard key is digital.

Analog means a whole range of values in between. A stick and a trigger are analog. This is what lets you walk slowly instead of sprinting, or gently feather a car's accelerator instead of flooring it.

Keep this word. It's the reason for a design decision we'll make in Chapter 3 that costs us real effort.

Why this is harder than it sounds

Here's the naive plan almost everyone thinks of first:

"When the phone says A was pressed, my PC program presses the Space key. Done."

You can build that in an afternoon, and it will genuinely work for some games. But it hits a wall fast:

  • No analog. Your sticks now only do full-speed-or-nothing. Every game becomes sprint-or-stand-still.
  • Wrong prompts. The game shows "Press Space to jump" instead of "Press Ⓐ to jump," because as far as it knows, you're on a keyboard.
  • Many games just ignore it. Some read input in a way that bypasses fake keystrokes entirely.
  • Steam won't see a controller. No Big Picture navigation, no controller configuration, none of it.

So the real goal is more demanding than it first appeared. We don't want to simulate someone using a keyboard. We want the computer to genuinely believe a controller is plugged into it.

The actual target

Not "make the game respond to my phone." Instead: make Windows itself believe there is a real Xbox controller physically attached to this PC.

Get that, and every game works automatically, forever, with no per-game effort. Chapter 2 explains why that's possible at all, and Chapter 3 explains how.

Wait — isn't there an app that already does this?context

Yes, several. The best-known is DroidJoy, a closed-source Windows + Android product. It supports up to four phones at once, has a full button layout, and does exactly what this book describes. There are open-source ones too — PadConnect, Joy2DroidX, VirtualGamePad — and we'll dissect all of them in Chapter 12.

So why build your own? Because the interesting part isn't the product, it's the layers. This project touches touchscreen input handling, real-time networking, binary protocol design, and Windows kernel drivers — a genuinely broad slice of computing, all in service of something you can hold in your hands and play a game with. It's an unusually good thing to learn on.

And there's real room to beat them. By Chapter 12 you'll see specific things every one of these projects is missing.

What you now know
  1. The system is two programs — a client on the phone, a server on the PC — plus the gap between them.
  2. Controllers don't send events ("A was pressed"). They send state — a complete snapshot of every button and stick, over and over, ~125 times a second.
  3. State-based reporting is self-healing: if a snapshot goes missing, the next one carries the full truth anyway.
  4. There are three kinds of input: digital buttons, and analog sticks and triggers.
  5. Analog input is why faking keyboard presses isn't good enough.
  6. The real goal is to make Windows believe a physical controller is attached — not to simulate a person typing.
