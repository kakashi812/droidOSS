Chapter Six building

Where the milliseconds go

A gamepad that responds late is worthless. So let's find out — precisely — what makes it late, and which of those things you can actually influence.

Builds on Ch 5: data crosses from phone to server through a socket.

Why this chapter exists

Input lag is the one quality bar this project has to clear. A controller that responds 200 ms after your thumb is not a controller, it's a novelty. So it's worth being rigorous rather than superstitious about where the delay comes from.

This chapter exists mostly to prevent one specific, extremely common mistake: spending weeks optimizing the layer that was never the problem.

Millisecond (ms)

One thousandth of a second. For calibration: a screen refreshing at 60 Hz draws a new frame every 16.7 ms. Most people begin to notice input lag somewhere around 50–70 ms, and competitive players notice far less.

Our budget is roughly under 50 ms end to end. That's comfortably achievable — but only if we spend it in the right places.

The uncomfortable conclusion

Look at that orange sliver. Four pixels out of six hundred and forty.

Deserializing twenty bytes and calling one function is genuinely a matter of microseconds — millionths of a second — in any language invented in the last forty years. A hand-tuned C server and a garbage-collected C# server produce input lag that differs by an amount no human can perceive, and that most instruments would struggle to separate from ordinary Wi-Fi jitter.

The mistake this chapter prevents

The reasoning goes: "gamepad → low latency matters → I must write this in a systems language." It sounds rigorous. It optimizes the one layer that was never the bottleneck, while the actual costs sit untouched in the network and the touchscreen.

This isn't hypothetical. In Chapter 12 we'll look at a real project whose server was rewritten across four different languages — while the layer that genuinely determined its quality stayed broken the whole time.

Think of it this way

You're trying to shorten a two-hour journey made up of a 90-minute flight, a 25-minute taxi, and a 4-second walk to the front door.

Optimising your code is sprinting the last four seconds. You could double your speed and it would round to nothing. The flight is the network; the taxi is the game's frame rate.

The one number you do choose: send rate

How often should the phone send its snapshot? This is a real decision with a real trade-off, and it's the one piece of the budget genuinely in your hands.

Why fixed-rate beats event-driven, in detailworth understanding

Event-driven means: send a packet whenever the input changes. It seems obviously better — no wasted packets when nothing's happening.

Two problems.

Bursts. Your thumb sliding across a stick generates touch events irregularly — sometimes several in a millisecond, sometimes a gap. Forwarding each one produces clumpy traffic. The receiving end sees a flurry, then silence, then a flurry. Even though the average latency is fine, the variation is what your hand notices, and it reads as the controller feeling loose or unpredictable.

Jitter

Not how long the delay is, but how much it varies. A steady 30 ms delay feels far better than one bouncing randomly between 10 and 40 ms — your hand adapts to consistency but cannot adapt to randomness.

Silence is ambiguous. With event-driven sending, "no packets" could mean nothing changed — or that the phone died. You'd need a separate heartbeat message to tell those apart. With fixed-rate sending, the stream is the heartbeat: silence unambiguously means something is wrong. That turns out to matter a lot in Chapter 11.

And the cost of sending constantly? 20 bytes × 125 per second = 2.5 KB/s. A single low-quality photo is a hundred times that. It's free.

What you now know
  1. Total budget is around 34 ms, and it should stay under roughly 50 ms to feel right.
  2. Your own code is about 0.2 ms of that — under 1%. Language choice is essentially irrelevant to latency.
  3. The real costs are the touchscreen, the network, and the game's frame rate — two of which you don't control at all.
  4. The highest-leverage fix is 5 GHz Wi-Fi, which is documentation rather than code.
  5. Send at a fixed rate, not on events — steady beats bursty, and it doubles as a heartbeat.
  6. 125 Hz (every 8 ms) is the right rate; it matches a real USB gamepad.
  7. Jitter — variation in delay — hurts more than a slightly higher constant delay.
