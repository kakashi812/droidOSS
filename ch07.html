Choosing how to speak: UDP vs TCP

There are two ways to send data over a network. One guarantees delivery. For us, that guarantee is a liability — and understanding why teaches something that generalises well beyond this project.

Builds on Ch 6: jitter hurts more than constant delay. And Ch 1: controllers send complete state, not events. That second one finally pays off here.

The two options

When you create a socket, you pick a protocol. In practice there are two, and they make opposite promises.

Read those columns cold and TCP looks strictly better. It's what websites, email, and file downloads use — for good reason. If you're downloading a photo, a missing chunk means a corrupted photo, so waiting is obviously correct.

We're going to choose UDP. Here's why that isn't reckless.

What "guaranteed delivery" actually costs

TCP's promise is that data arrives in order. The only way to keep that promise is: when something goes missing, stop and wait for it — and hold back everything that arrived behind it, because delivering that would break the ordering guarantee.

The line worth remembering

Losing a packet costs us nothing. Waiting for one costs us everything.

That's only true because of the decision made back in Chapter 1 — that we send complete state rather than events. Because every packet is self-sufficient, the next one 8 ms later contains the entire truth. There is literally nothing to recover.

Think of it this way

TCP is registered post. Every letter arrives, in order, signed for. If one goes missing the post office holds up your entire mail delivery until it's found — including the letters already sitting in the depot.

UDP is shouting across a room. Some words get lost in the noise. But you're repeating yourself 125 times a second, so it doesn't matter — and crucially, you never stop and wait for someone to confirm they heard the last one.

Why events would have forced our hand

Chapter 1 made a point of distinguishing state from events, and promised it would matter. Here's the payoff.

The one thing you must add

UDP can deliver packets out of order. If snapshot #58 arrives after #59, your stick briefly jumps backwards to an older position — a visible glitch.

The fix is four lines. Number every packet, and ignore anything that isn't newer than what you've already seen.

if (packet.seq <= lastSeq) return;   // stale or duplicate — bin it
lastSeq = packet.seq;
ApplyState(packet);
Sequence number

A counter that increases by one with every packet sent. Its only purpose is to let the receiver tell "newer" from "older." We'll use a 32-bit number, which at 125 packets per second takes about 1.1 years of continuous play to run out.

Being strictly correct about counter wrap-aroundpedantry, but real

After roughly 1.1 years of continuous play, the counter wraps back to zero. At that instant, packet.seq <= lastSeq is true for every subsequent packet, and your controller stops responding forever.

Nobody will hit this. But the correct version costs nothing:

if ((int)(packet.seq - lastSeq) <= 0) return;

Subtracting unsigned values and interpreting the result as signed handles the wrap correctly — the difference comes out as a small positive number even across the boundary. It's a standard trick worth having in your head; you'll meet it again in anything involving rolling counters.

What about Bluetooth? DroidJoy supports itscope decision

It does, and you should still skip it for now.

  • It's slower. Bluetooth serial adds meaningful latency versus 5 GHz Wi-Fi, so you'd be working hard for a worse result.
  • Android's Bluetooth stack is genuinely unpleasant. Pairing flows, permission changes across Android versions, and inconsistent device behaviour. You'd spend your time fighting the platform rather than learning the architecture.
  • You can't inspect it. With Wi-Fi you can watch every packet in Wireshark and test the server with a Python script. With Bluetooth you're debugging blind.

Its one genuine advantage is working with no Wi-Fi network present. Worth adding eventually — just not while you're still learning the shape of the system.

What you now know
  1. TCP guarantees delivery and ordering, and keeps that promise by stalling everything when data goes missing.
  2. UDP guarantees nothing and never waits.
  3. For realtime input, a re-sent packet is stale and worthless — it describes a moment that has passed.
  4. Losing a packet costs nothing; waiting for one costs everything.
  5. Sending state rather than events is precisely what makes unreliable delivery acceptable. The two decisions are one decision.
  6. Add a sequence number and discard anything not newer — that's the only thing UDP asks of you.
